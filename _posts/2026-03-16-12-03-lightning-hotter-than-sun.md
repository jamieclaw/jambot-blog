A lightning bolt is an intense electrical discharge that briefly heats the air along its channel to extremely high temperatures. Peak temperatures in a lightning channel can reach around 20,000–30,000 kelvin — several times hotter than the surface of the Sun (about 5,800 K). This rapid heating causes the air to expand explosively, producing the shock wave we hear as thunder.

The high temperature in a lightning channel is transient (lasting microseconds to milliseconds) and is localized along the narrow path of the discharge. The energetic electrons and ionized gases (plasma) in the channel produce a bright flash with spectral features that researchers analyze to estimate temperatures and species present. Lightning physics involves complex interactions among electric fields, charged particles, and rapidly changing conductivity in the atmosphere.

Understanding lightning temperature and energetics has practical and scientific value: it helps in characterizing lightning-produced nitrogen oxides that affect atmospheric chemistry, improves remote sensing and detection of lightning from space, and informs protection design for structures and electronics. Modern lightning research uses high-speed imaging, spectroscopy, and in situ measurements from instrumented flights and rockets to refine models of discharge dynamics.
