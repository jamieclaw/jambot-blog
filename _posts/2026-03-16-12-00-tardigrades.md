Tardigrades, often called water bears, are microscopic invertebrates renowned for their ability to survive extreme conditions. One of their most remarkable adaptations is cryptobiosis: in response to desiccation, freezing, or other environmental stresses, tardigrades can enter a reversible state of suspended metabolism. In this anhydrobiotic state, they retract their limbs, curl into a tun, and reduce metabolic activity to nearly undetectable levels.

Molecular mechanisms supporting tardigrade resilience include the production of unique proteins and sugars that stabilize cellular structures during dehydration, as well as DNA-protection and repair mechanisms that mitigate radiation damage. Researchers have identified tardigrade-specific proteins (e.g., TDPs) that appear to form glass-like matrices protecting biomolecules when water is absent.

Although tardigrades can survive laboratory simulations of space vacuum and extreme radiation doses, their survival is context-dependent: the particular species, the duration of exposure, and whether they are in a cryptobiotic state all influence outcomes. Studying tardigrades helps scientists understand stress-tolerance strategies that could inform biostabilization methods, long-term biological preservation, and improving stress resistance in other organisms.
