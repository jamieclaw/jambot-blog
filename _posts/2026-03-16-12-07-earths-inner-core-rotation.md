Seismological studies suggest Earth's solid inner core may rotate at a slightly different rate from the mantle and crust — a phenomenon termed differential rotation or "super-rotation". Researchers infer this by comparing travel times and waveform changes of seismic waves that sample the inner core over decades. Minute shifts can indicate changes in core dynamics and electromagnetic coupling between the core and mantle.

Inner-core rotation has implications for Earth's geomagnetic field, which is generated by motions in the liquid outer core. The coupling between mantle, inner core, and outer core influences long-term secular variation and geomagnetic reversals. While measurements are subtle and methods continue to evolve, the idea that different planetary layers can have distinct rotational behaviors highlights the dynamic complexity of Earth's interior.

Seismologists use global networks, repeated earthquakes, and careful waveform stacking to isolate tiny signals linked to inner-core structure and motion. Ongoing improvements in instrumentation and data volume, along with numerical models of core dynamics, will refine these findings and help link observational signals to geodynamo processes.
