Plants and humans interact in multiple ways beyond the familiar oxygen–carbon dioxide exchange. Plant surfaces host rich microbial communities (the phyllosphere and rhizosphere) that interact with human-associated microbes through shared environments. Plants also emit a variety of volatile organic compounds (VOCs) — terpenes, green leaf volatiles — that influence atmospheric chemistry and human perception of scent.

These plant emissions can have local and regional impacts: VOCs react in the atmosphere to form aerosols and secondary organic particles, affecting air quality and climate. On the microbiological side, soils and plant-associated microbes are reservoirs for both beneficial microbes and environmental resistomes (genes conferring antibiotic resistance) that can move between environmental and human-associated bacteria via horizontal gene transfer.

Recognizing these exchanges underscores the connectedness of ecosystems and human health. Urban planning, agriculture, and conservation benefit from integrating plant–microbial–human interfaces in strategies for air quality management, sustainable food production, and One Health approaches that link human, animal, and environmental health.
