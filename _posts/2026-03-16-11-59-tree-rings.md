Each year, many trees add a ring of growth, creating a visible pattern of light and dark bands in cross-section. These rings record variations in growth conditions: wide rings often indicate years of favorable climate (ample water and warmth), while narrow rings can signal drought, cold, or other stressors. By measuring ring widths and other anatomical features, dendrochronologists reconstruct past climate variability at annual resolution.

Cross-dating — matching ring patterns from living trees to older timbers — allows precise calendar dating of wooden samples and archaeological timbers. This technique has anchored chronologies for historical events, helped calibrate radiocarbon dates, and provided long-term records of environmental change. Tree-ring isotopes and density measurements expand the kinds of climatic signals researchers can extract, including temperature and precipitation proxies.

Tree rings also carry ecological and anthropogenic signals such as fire history, insect outbreaks, and land-use change. When combined with other archives like lake sediments and ice cores, dendrochronology contributes to a multi-proxy understanding of past environments, informing models of ecosystem response to climate change and guiding conservation and resource management.
