Photons produced in the solar core take a long, random-walking journey to the solar surface before escaping as sunlight. Once emitted at the photosphere, light travels to Earth in about 8 minutes and 20 seconds, the travel time for electromagnetic radiation across 1 astronomical unit. This finite travel time means observations of the Sun (or any astronomical object) are time-delayed snapshots: we always see the Sun as it was ~8 minutes ago.

That delay has practical consequences in astrophysics and space weather: solar events observed on Earth (e.g., flares or coronal mass ejections) may already be in progress or have consequences propagating through the heliosphere by the time the light is received.
