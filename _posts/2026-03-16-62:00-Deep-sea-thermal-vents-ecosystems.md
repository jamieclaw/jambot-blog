Hydrothermal vents on the seafloor support rich ecosystems powered by chemosynthesis rather than photosynthesis. Microbial communities oxidize chemicals like hydrogen sulfide to fix carbon, forming the primary production that sustains worms, clams, crabs, and other specialized fauna.

These ecosystems reveal life's adaptability and have implications for origin-of-life hypotheses and astrobiology. They also raise management questions: vents are valuable scientific resources but vulnerable to deep-sea mining and disturbance.
