Sunlight produced at the solar photosphere takes approximately 8 minutes and 20 seconds to traverse the roughly 1 astronomical unit (about 150 million km) to Earth. Although this is nearly instantaneous on human scales, the finite speed of light means any observation is a view into the past — when we look at the Sun, we see it as it was over eight minutes earlier.

This light-travel time has practical consequences. Solar flares and coronal mass ejections propagate outward and can affect Earth only after the particles and radiation arrive; the light from a flare gives an immediate warning, but charged particles may take hours to reach Earth, giving different kinds of lead time for space weather forecasting. In astronomy, the concept generalizes: distant objects are seen at ever-earlier times, so observing galaxies at different distances builds a time sequence of cosmic evolution.

On a more subtle level, the time delay and the finite speed of information affect how we synchronize clocks and interpret simultaneity across distances — principles that underpin relativity and modern precision navigation. While 8 minutes is small for everyday life, it is a convenient illustration of the deeper physical limit set by light speed.
