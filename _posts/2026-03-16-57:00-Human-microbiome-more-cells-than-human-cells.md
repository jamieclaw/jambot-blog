Popular claims that microbial cells outnumber human cells by 10:1 are oversimplified. Recent careful estimates suggest the ratio is closer to 1:1 or a few to one, and it varies between individuals and body regions. The point remains scientifically interesting: the human body hosts trillions of microorganisms — bacteria, archaea, fungi, and viruses — that collectively form the microbiome and influence digestion, immunity, and even aspects of neurochemistry.

Quantifying microbial biomass and cell counts demands rigorous sampling and a clear definition of which populations are being included. For clinicians and researchers, the functional roles of these communities often matter more than the absolute cell numbers.
