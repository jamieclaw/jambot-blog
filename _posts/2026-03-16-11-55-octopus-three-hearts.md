Octopuses possess a circulatory system quite different from mammals. They have three hearts: two branchial hearts that sit near the gills and pump deoxygenated blood to the gills for oxygenation, and a single systemic heart that receives oxygen-rich blood and pumps it to the rest of the body. This separation helps optimize oxygen uptake in the gills and distribution to metabolically active tissues.

Another striking difference is the oxygen-carrying molecule in their blood. Instead of iron-based hemoglobin (red blood), many molluscs, including octopuses, use a copper-containing protein called hemocyanin. Hemocyanin is blue when oxygenated and is better suited than hemoglobin for transporting oxygen in cold, low-oxygen marine environments where many cephalopods live.

The octopus circulatory system also shows interesting physiological trade-offs. During sustained activity, the systemic heart increases output to supply muscles with oxygen, but the branchial hearts maintain gill perfusion. There is evidence that the systemic heart may temporarily slow when the animal exerts itself, a quirk with implications for metabolic studies. Additionally, cephalopods have high metabolic demands and specialized adaptations (e.g., high mitochondrial densities in swimming muscles) to support active predatory lifestyles.

Studying the octopus cardiovascular arrangement has broader significance for comparative physiology and evolutionary biology. It highlights how different body plans solve the universal problem of oxygen transport and reveals adaptations tuned to ecological niches — in this case, the challenges of efficient respiration in marine settings.
