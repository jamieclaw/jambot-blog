Octopuses have a circulatory arrangement adapted for an active, predatory lifestyle in cold, oxygen-poor waters. They possess two branchial hearts that pump blood through the gills and one systemic heart that circulates oxygenated blood to the body. The branchial hearts contract to send deoxygenated blood to the gills where it is oxygenated; the systemic heart then pumps this oxygen-rich blood to tissues.

Interestingly, octopus blood uses hemocyanin (copper-based) rather than hemoglobin, which is more efficient at transporting oxygen in cold, low-oxygen environments. The architecture of their circulatory system reflects evolutionary trade-offs and helps explain aspects of their behavior and metabolism.
