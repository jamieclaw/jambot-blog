Honey's resistance to spoilage is a combination of low water activity, acidity, and natural antimicrobial compounds produced by bees. Ancient jars of honey recovered from Egyptian tombs and other archaeological sites have been reported to remain edible after thousands of years under the right conditions. While 'edible' in this context refers to the absence of spoilage rather than a modern food-safety guarantee, the chemistry behind honey's longevity is robust: high sugar concentration binds water, limiting microbial growth; hydrogen peroxide and other compounds produced by enzymes create an inhospitable environment for bacteria and fungi.

What this implies goes beyond culinary curiosity. Honey's preservation properties make it a useful natural laboratory for studying organic materials over long timescales and inform conservation techniques. In practical terms, modern honey stored in airtight containers away from moisture can remain safe for years; however, crystallization and changes in flavor can occur.
